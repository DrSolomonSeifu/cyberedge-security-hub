Lesson Overview

What happens when prevention is not enough

Earlier lessons focused on building and evaluating defenses. This lesson assumes that, sooner or later, something will get through. When it does, an organization needs a disciplined way to respond, and continuous monitoring that surfaces the problem in the first place.

Lesson 12 brings four connected ideas together: how incidents are handled end to end, how digital evidence is preserved, where the data that reveals an attack comes from, and which tools turn that data into timely alerts.

💡 Key Idea
Detection and response are two halves of one capability. Monitoring without a response plan produces alerts no one acts on; a response plan without monitoring never gets triggered. A mature security operation connects data → alert → response → lessons learned into a single continuous loop.

Topic 12A · Incident Response

Handling an incident from first signal to final review

📖 Definition
An event is any observable occurrence on a system or network. An incident is an event that actually threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information. Incident response is the organized process of detecting, managing, and recovering from incidents.

The Incident Response Lifecycle

Incident response follows a repeatable lifecycle. It is drawn as a loop because the final step feeds straight back into the first.

1

Preparation

Build the plan, the team, the tools, and the communication paths before anything happens.

2

Detection

Recognize that something is wrong, from monitoring tools or from people reporting it.

3

Analysis

Confirm the incident, determine its scope and severity, and prioritize the response.

4

Containment

Limit the spread and stop the damage from growing, often by isolating affected systems.

5

Eradication

Remove the threat itself — malware, accounts, footholds — from the environment.

6

Recovery

Restore systems to normal operation and verify they are clean before returning them to service.

7

Lessons Learned

Review what happened, fix the gaps, and feed improvements back into preparation.

Preparation in Depth

💡 Key Idea
The best response work is done before the incident. Preparation means having the people, paperwork, and tooling ready so the team is following a plan, not inventing one under pressure.
  • Incident Response Plan (IRP) — the documented procedures the team follows, defining roles, steps, and decision authority.
  • Response team (CSIRT/CIRT) — the named people responsible for handling incidents, with clear roles.
  • Communication plan — who is notified, how, and in what order, including out-of-band channels in case normal systems are down.
  • Tools and infrastructure — forensic kit, secure evidence storage, and isolated environments ready in advance.

Detection and Analysis

Detection sources range from automated tools to alert users. Many attacks — phishing, social engineering, odd system behavior — are first noticed by people, which is why awareness training treats every user as a sensor.

🔎 Analysis Tools
The cyber kill chain maps an attack into stages, helping responders understand how far an attacker progressed. Playbooks give step-by-step response procedures for specific incident types, so the team responds consistently. Analysis also includes triage — ranking incidents by functional impact, information impact, recoverability, and scope.

Containment, Eradication, and Recovery

🛑

Containment

Stop the bleeding. Short-term isolation limits spread; long-term containment keeps operations running while a permanent fix is built.

🧹

Eradication

Remove the root cause — malware, malicious accounts, and persistence — so the threat cannot simply return.

♻️

Recovery

Restore from clean backups, validate integrity, and monitor closely as systems return to production.

Lessons Learned

💡 Why It Matters
After the incident is over, the team holds a post-incident review. A common technique is the Five Whys — asking "why" repeatedly to move past the symptom and reach the true root cause. The output is concrete improvements that flow back into the preparation step, closing the loop.

Beyond the Reactive Loop

  • Testing and training — tabletop exercises and simulations rehearse the plan so the team is ready before a real incident.
  • Threat hunting — proactively searching the environment for hidden threats, rather than waiting for an alert.
📌 Example
A SIEM alert flags ransomware encrypting files on one server (detection). The team confirms it and finds three affected hosts (analysis), isolates them from the network (containment), wipes and rebuilds them (eradication), restores data from clean backups (recovery), and then runs a Five Whys review that reveals an unpatched VPN appliance as the entry point — which becomes a new preparation task (lessons learned).

Topic 12B · Digital Forensics

Preserving evidence that holds up under scrutiny

📖 Definition
Digital forensics is the discipline of identifying, collecting, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence in a way that is both analytically reliable and legally defensible.
💡 Key Idea
Forensics serves two goals at once: understanding what happened, and producing evidence that could stand up in court or a regulatory investigation. These goals create tension — the fastest way to fix a system (wiping and reimaging it) can destroy the very evidence you need. Good responders document every action and protect evidence even under pressure.

Legal Foundations

  • Due process — evidence must be handled fairly and lawfully to be usable.
  • Legal hold — once litigation is anticipated, relevant data must be preserved and not deleted.
  • E-discovery — the formal process of identifying and producing electronic evidence for legal proceedings.

Acquisition and the Order of Volatility

When collecting evidence, capture the most fragile data first. Anything in memory disappears when power is lost, so it is collected before data that persists on disk.

Most volatileCPU cache & registers
→
ThenSystem memory (RAM)
→
ThenNetwork state & connections
→
ThenDisk & storage
→
Least volatileBackups & archives
🧠

System Memory Acquisition

Capture a live image of RAM to preserve running processes, open connections, encryption keys, and malware that never touches the disk.

💽

Disk Image Acquisition

Make a bit-for-bit copy of storage using a write blocker, then hash it so any later change to the original or copy is detectable.

Preservation and Chain of Custody

⚠ Chain of Custody
Chain of custody is the documented record of who handled the evidence, when, and why, from collection to courtroom. A broken chain can make valid evidence inadmissible — cases have been dismissed not because the evidence was wrong, but because no one documented who accessed it. Hashing the evidence proves it was not altered.

Reporting and E-Discovery

The investigation ends with a clear, factual report of what was found, how, and what it means. That report, together with the preserved evidence, supports e-discovery if the incident moves into legal or regulatory proceedings.

📌 Example
Responders arrive at a compromised workstation that is still powered on. They first capture a memory image (preserving the active malware and its network connections), then attach a write blocker and image the disk, hashing both. Every transfer of the drive is logged and signed. Months later in court, the matching hashes and complete custody log prove the evidence is exactly as collected.

Topic 12C · Data Sources

Where the evidence of an attack actually lives

💡 Key Idea
You can only detect what you can see. Monitoring and investigation both depend on rich data sources — and a single source rarely tells the whole story. The skill is knowing which source answers which question, and correlating across them.

Dashboards and Reports

Dashboards turn streams of raw data into a visual summary of security posture at a glance, while scheduled reports communicate trends and compliance status to leadership over time.

Log Data

Logs are the foundation of detection. Different logs answer different questions:

Log SourceWhat It Reveals
Host / OS logsLogons, privilege use, account changes, and system events on a machine.
Application logsWhat an application did — errors, transactions, and user actions inside it.
Endpoint logsProcess activity, file changes, and behavior captured by endpoint protection.
Network logsFirewall, IDS/IPS, and device logs showing what traffic crossed the network.

Network Data Sources

🌊

NetFlow

Summarizes who talked to whom, how much, and for how long — without storing the full content of the traffic.

📦

Packet Captures

Record the full contents of network traffic for deep analysis, the most detailed and most storage-heavy source.

🏷️

Metadata

Data about data — timestamps, file origins, email headers, and senders that reveal context an attacker may overlook.

🔎 Flow vs Capture
NetFlow is like a phone bill: it shows the calls, durations, and numbers, but not the conversation. A packet capture is the recording of the call itself. Flow data is cheap to keep at scale for spotting patterns; full captures are reserved for the moments that need deep inspection.
📌 Example
An analyst notices in NetFlow that one workstation is sending large, regular bursts of data to an unfamiliar overseas address every hour — a possible beacon. The flow record raised the flag cheaply; a targeted packet capture then confirms command-and-control traffic hidden inside the connection.

Topic 12D · Alerting and Monitoring Tools

Turning oceans of data into timely, trustworthy alerts

💡 Key Idea
Enterprises generate far more log data than any human can read. Monitoring tools exist to do the data reduction and pattern recognition that make analysis possible — and to raise an alert at the right moment without burying the team in noise.

SIEM — The Command Center

📖 Definition
Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) aggregates log data from across the enterprise into one place, then correlates events from many sources to reveal attack sequences that are invisible in any single log.

SIEM's core value is correlation: linking a failed-login spike on one system to a firewall log on another to flag a brute-force attempt that neither source would show alone.

Splunk IBM QRadar Microsoft Sentinel

Core Monitoring Activities

🔔

Alerting

Generating notifications when activity matches a rule or crosses a threshold, so analysts can act.

📊

Reporting

Summarizing security activity over time for trends, audits, and compliance evidence.

🗄️

Archiving

Retaining log data securely for the period required by policy and regulation, for later investigation.

Alert Tuning

⚠ Alert Fatigue
An untuned system floods analysts with false positives — alerts that are not real threats. Over time the team stops trusting alerts, and a real one slips past. Alert tuning refines rules and priority values for the specific environment, cutting noise so that the alerts that fire actually matter.

Monitoring Infrastructure, Systems, and Applications

  • Infrastructure — protocols like SNMP and flow data like NetFlow watch the health and traffic of network devices.
  • Systems — monitoring agents track the performance and security state of servers and endpoints.
  • Applications — application monitoring watches for errors, abuse, and anomalies inside software.

Benchmarks and SCAP

🔎 Measuring Against a Standard
Monitoring is not only about live activity — it also checks systems against a known-good baseline. The Security Content Automation Protocol (SCAP) lets tools automatically test configurations against benchmarks, so drift away from the secure standard is detected continuously rather than during an audit.

The Supporting Tool Stack

🛰️

IDS / IPS

Detect (IDS) or block (IPS) malicious traffic by signature and behavior. Snort and Suricata are common.

💻

EDR

Endpoint Detection and Response watches process behavior to catch threats with no known signature, and can isolate a host remotely.

⚙️

SOAR & Automation

Orchestrates and automates response with playbooks, so well-understood actions happen at machine speed.

📝 Final Lesson Takeaway
Monitoring tools are the eyes of the security operation, and SIEM is where those eyes come together. But tools only matter if their alerts are tuned to be trustworthy and connected to a response process that acts on them. Data sources supply the evidence, forensics preserves its integrity, and incident response turns all of it into action — and then into a stronger defense the next time.
