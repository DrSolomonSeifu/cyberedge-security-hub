⚡ Mission Brief — Who Is Attacking Us and How?

On June 27, 2017, NotPetya malware — disguised as a ransomware attack — spread globally within hours. It crippled shipping giant Maersk, pharmaceutical firm Merck, and logistics company FedEx. Total damage: over $10 billion. It was later attributed to a Russian nation-state threat actor.

This was not a random attack. It was a structured, targeted, multistage campaign — executed by a sophisticated actor with clear political motivation, vast resources, and a specific attack vector: a compromised software update mechanism in Ukrainian accounting software.

Every concept in this lesson — threat actor types, motivations, attack surfaces, and social engineering techniques — exists to help defenders recognize and respond to exactly this kind of threat. Know your adversary. Know their path. Know their playbook.
🎯 Lesson Objectives
  • Objective 1: Compare and contrast attributes and motivations of threat actor types — from script kiddies to nation-states.
  • Objective 2: Explain common threat vectors and attack surfaces — software, network, lure-based, message-based, and supply chain.
  • Objective 3: Identify social engineering techniques — impersonation, phishing, pharming, typosquatting, and BEC.
  • Objective 4: Apply threat knowledge to make more accurate and effective security assessments.

📋 Threat Actor Attributes

Traditional cybersecurity relied on identifying static known threats — viruses, Trojans, botnets. Adversaries learned to bypass signature-based detection. Modern security requires building behavioral profiles of threat actors across four key attributes.

📍

Location

Internal or external access before the attack begins.

⚙️

Capability

Sophistication level — commodity tools to novel exploits.

💰

Resources

Funding level — lone actor to nation-state backing.

🎯

Motivation

Chaotic, financial, or political goals driving the attack.

🎭 Motivations in Detail

🌀 Chaotic Motivations

  • Causing disruption for its own sake
  • Website defacement and worm releases
  • Revenge attacks by employees or ex-employees
  • Supporting political ends or war aims

💰 Financial Motivations

  • Blackmail — payment to suppress information
  • Extortion — payment to halt an ongoing attack (ransomware)
  • Fraud — falsifying records to steal funds or launder money
  • Selling stolen data to third parties

🌐 Political Motivations

  • Whistleblowers exposing wrongdoing
  • Hacktivists disrupting target organizations
  • Nation-state espionage between governments
  • Disinformation campaigns to influence society
🎩 Hackers, Hacktivists, and Teams

🎩 Hacker Types

Authorized (White Hat)

Always seeks permission before testing. Work is legal, ethical, and sanctioned. Example: a penetration tester hired to find weaknesses before attackers do.

Unauthorized (Black Hat)

Gains access without permission. Actions are illegal and typically malicious or self-serving.

Script Kiddie

Uses pre-built tools without understanding how they work. No specific target or strategy. Even low-skill attackers can cause real damage against weak defenses.

✊ Hacktivists and Teams

Hacker Teams

Modern threat actors frequently operate as organized teams — not lone individuals. Collaboration allows development of sophisticated tools, novel strategies, and specialized division of labor.

Hacktivists

Use cyber weapons to promote political or ideological agendas. Common targets: political organizations, media companies, financial institutions, and companies targeted by advocacy groups. Techniques: data exfiltration, service disruption, and website defacement.

🏴 Nation-State Actors and APTs

🏴 Nation-State Actors and Advanced Persistent Threats

What Makes Them Unique

  • Attached to military or secret services — but operate at arm's length
  • Maintain plausible deniability — sponsors claim no official involvement
  • May pose as independent groups or hacktivists
  • May wage false flag campaigns to implicate other nations

What They Target

  • Energy infrastructure, healthcare systems, electoral processes
  • Primary goals: espionage and disinformation for strategic advantage
  • Some nations — e.g., North Korea — also target organizations for financial gain

APT — Advanced Persistent Threat

Traditional security thought in terms of isolated infections. APT shifts the focus to sustained, ongoing compromise — gaining access, maintaining it over time, and using diverse tools to avoid detection.

MITRE ATT&CK

The MITRE Corporation maintains a publicly accessible database of known threat groups — IDs, names, aliases, tactics, and targets. An essential resource for mapping observed behaviors to known threat actor profiles.

🏢 Organized Crime, Competitors, and Insiders

💼 Organized Crime

Motivated purely by criminal profit. Operate across national borders — making prosecution legally complex. Primary activities: financial fraud against individuals and companies, blackmail, and extortion (ransomware).

In many countries, cybercrime has now surpassed physical crime in total incidents and financial losses.

🤝 Competitors

Businesses targeting rivals through cyber means — though less common than criminal attacks. Goals: stealing trade secrets, disrupting operations, or damaging reputation. Often enabled by employees who recently changed companies and bring insider knowledge.

Employee transitions — both departures and new hires — represent a security risk that must be actively managed.

👤 Insider Threats

Actors already granted authorized access. Permanent insiders (employees) or temporary insiders (contractors, guests). Former employees are a blurred case — may retain residual access if offboarding was inadequate.

Unintentional Insiders

Caused by lack of awareness or carelessness. Poor password management, policy violations, and shadow IT — unauthorized devices or cloud services introduced without IT approval.

Note: Whistleblowers making protected disclosures are legally protected — they cannot be labeled or penalized retaliatorily.
🌐 Attack Surface and Vectors

🗺️ What Is the Attack Surface?

The attack surface is every point where a malicious threat actor could attempt to exploit a vulnerability. It includes any location or method where a threat actor can interact with a network port, application, computer, or user.

Attack Surface Can Be Assessed At:

  • The entire organization — broadest view
  • A single server or computer — system level
  • A specific web application
  • Employee identities and accounts — human layer
Key Insight: A sophisticated threat actor's knowledge of your attack surface may be more complete than your own. They may have already mapped vulnerabilities your team has not yet discovered.

Minimizing the Attack Surface Means:

  • Allowing only known, necessary endpoints
  • Permitting only required protocols and ports
  • Exposing only essential services
  • Continuously monitoring all permitted access points

💻 Vulnerable Software Vectors

⚠️ Vulnerable Software

Software containing a code or design flaw that bypasses access controls or crashes a process. Almost no modern software is vulnerability-free — complexity and release pressure make this unavoidable.

Two Problems Persist

  • Organizations may lack an effective patch management process.
  • There is always a window between discovery and deployment of a patch.

Reducing the Software Attack Surface

  • Consolidation — fewer different products in use
  • Standardization — same version deployed across the org

🔒 Unsupported Systems

An unsupported system is one whose vendor no longer releases updates or patches. Known vulnerabilities exist — but no official fix will ever come.

Strategy: Isolation — separate the vulnerable system from other systems to limit attacker pathways. This is a compensating control used in place of patching.

Scanning Approaches

  • Client-based (agent-based) — software installed on each host; deeper visibility.
  • Agentless — remote probe, no installation needed; preferred by threat actors during reconnaissance because it leaves minimal traces.
🔌 Network Vectors

🌐 Remote vs. Local Exploits

Remote Exploit: Triggered by sending code across the network. No authenticated session required. The attacker does not need credentials to launch the attack.
Local Exploit: Requires an authenticated session on the target system. The attack may still travel over a network — but valid credentials or a hijacked session are needed.

Unsecure Networks — CIA Triad Failures

  • No Confidentiality → Eavesdropping attacks — traffic is snooped, passwords captured.
  • No Integrity → On-path attacks — devices intercept and modify traffic in transit.
  • No Availability → Denial of Service (DoS) attacks — services disrupted for legitimate users.
🔌 Specific Network Attack Paths
🏢

Direct Access (Physical)

Threat actor gains physical access to the site — accesses unlocked workstations, uses boot disks to install tools, or physically steals hardware. Physical presence bypasses many network-level controls entirely.

🔌

Wired Network

Attacker connects an unauthorized device to a physical network port. If permitted to communicate, the device can launch eavesdropping, on-path, or DoS attacks from inside the perimeter.

📡

Remote and Wireless Network

Attacker obtains or cracks credentials for remote access or wireless connection. Or spoofs a trusted access point to harvest credentials from connecting users — then uses stolen details to access the real network.

☁️

Cloud Access

One weak account, service, or host is enough to gain a foothold. Attackers target developer accounts and cloud management accounts. May also attack the Cloud Service Provider (CSP) directly as a path into the victim's environment.

🦷

Bluetooth Network

Exploits a vulnerability or misconfiguration in the Bluetooth protocol to transmit a malicious file to a user's device over short range. Effective in crowded environments: offices, airports, conference venues.

🔑

Default Credentials

Device or application was never reconfigured after initial setup. Default usernames and passwords are publicly documented in product manuals. One of the most preventable — and most commonly exploited — vulnerabilities.

🚪

Open Service Ports

Attacker establishes an unauthenticated connection to an open TCP or UDP port. Applications listening on unnecessary open ports expand the attack surface. Servers should never be configured to accept traffic on ports not required for operations.

🪝 Lure-Based Vectors

🪝 What Is a Lure?

A lure appears attractive or legitimate on the surface — but conceals a malicious payload. When the target opens it, the payload typically gives the attacker control over the system or enables service disruption. Used when direct exploits are not possible — the attacker tricks the user into opening the door.

💾

Removable Devices

Malware on USB drives. Simply connecting the device may execute it. Drop attacks leave infected drives in offices, parking lots, or reception areas.

⚙️

Executable Files

Trojan Horse malware — appears as a free, useful program but silently installs a backdoor giving the attacker remote access.

📄

Document Files

Malicious code embedded in Word or PDF files. Exploits macro and scripting features — or vulnerabilities in the viewer software itself.

🖼️

Image Files

Exploit code concealed within an image. Targets vulnerabilities in the browser or document editor used to view the image. Users rarely expect images to be threats.

Defense: Effective endpoint security management — vulnerability management, antivirus, program execution control, and intrusion detection — is required to reduce this attack surface.
🔗 Supply Chain Attack Surface

🔗 Supply Chain

The supply chain is the end-to-end process of designing, manufacturing, and distributing goods and services. Rather than attack a well-defended target directly, attackers infiltrate through less-secured suppliers or partners.

Real-World Example: The Target data breach was executed using credentials belonging to Target's building systems vendor. A trusted third party became the attack entry point.

Key Relationships in Procurement

  • Supplier — obtains products from manufacturers; sells B2B in bulk.
  • Vendor — sells to businesses or consumers (B2B or B2C); may add support.
  • Business Partner — closer relationship with shared goals and deeper system access.

Why the Supply Chain Is So Dangerous

For a single piece of hardware to be trustworthy, every link in its chain must also be trustworthy — chip manufacturer, firmware developer, OEM reseller, courier, and administrative staff provisioning the device.

Securing the supply chain means denying malicious actors the time or resources to tamper with assets before they reach the organization.

MSP Risk — Managed Service Providers

MSPs provision and manage IT resources on behalf of organizations. Monitoring an MSP is difficult. All MSP employees are potential insider threats — they have authorized access to the client's environment.

🧠 Social Engineering — Hacking the Human

🧠 The Human Vector

Every organization's attack surface includes a human layer. Employees, contractors, suppliers, and customers — collectively called the human vector — are all potential targets. Social engineering exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities.

Technical defenses can be tested, patched, and hardened. Human behavior is far less predictable. Even technically sophisticated users can be manipulated if the right psychological triggers are activated — trust, authority, urgency, curiosity, or fear.

🎭 Impersonation and Pretexting

🎭 Impersonation

Pretending to be someone else. Most effective when the target cannot verify identity — over the phone or via email where there is no face, badge, or visual confirmation.

Two Approaches

Persuasive / Liking Approach

Makes the request feel natural and socially expected. Refusal feels impolite or unusual. The target complies because it feels like the right thing to do — not because they are forced.

"Hi, this is IT support. I just need your password to finish your account update — it'll take a second."

Coercion / Urgency Approach

Creates fear, false authority, or time pressure. Urgency removes the target's ability to pause, verify, or think critically before complying.

"This is the CIO's office. We have a live breach. I need your credentials now or the network goes down."

📜 Pretexting

A carefully crafted, detailed story that makes an impersonation more convincing. The attacker uses specific names, job titles, internal terminology, and organizational details.

Building an Effective Pretext

  • Research employee names, job titles, and department phone numbers
  • Collect diaries, invoices, meeting schedules, and purchase orders
  • Use internal terminology and processes to sound legitimate
Most organizations are structured around customer service — not security. This means reconnaissance information is often easy to obtain from company websites, LinkedIn, and front desk staff.
Defense requires policy — never share credentials verbally — plus consistent training that reinforces those policies under pressure.
🎣 Phishing, Vishing, SMiShing, and Pharming
📧

Phishing

Social engineering + spoofing via email. Tricks the target into interacting with a malicious resource disguised as a trusted one — to install malware, grant access, or harvest credentials via a spoofed website.

📞

Vishing

Voice phishing over telephone or VoIP. Harder to refuse a live voice request than an email. Deepfake technology is making voice and video phishing increasingly convincing.

💬

SMiShing

Phishing via SMS text message. SS7 protocol has known vulnerabilities. Organizations typically have no monitoring capability for SMS — making threats invisible to enterprise security tools.

🔗

Pharming

Corrupts the DNS name resolution process to silently redirect users from legitimate websites to malicious ones. No user action needed beyond typing the correct address — they are redirected without knowing.

⚡

Zero-Click Exploits

The most dangerous escalation. Simply receiving a message or viewing a file triggers the exploit. No user action required. Even trained, security-aware users cannot prevent it through behavior alone.

🚗

Drive-By Download

A compromised website automatically infects visiting browsers with a vulnerable application. No deliberate click or download needed — simply visiting the page triggers the exploit.

🔤 Typosquatting and Email Spoofing

🔤 Typosquatting

Registering a domain name that closely mimics a legitimate one through subtle misspellings or character substitutions. The goal is to go unnoticed — the difference is subtle enough that most users will not catch it.

Examples: exannple.com instead of example.com — the extra "n" is nearly invisible at a glance.

Also Known As

  • Cousin domains
  • Lookalike domains
  • Doppelganger domains

Hijacked Subdomains

Registers a subdomain under a trusted cloud provider's root domain — e.g., example.onmicrosoft.com — borrowing the provider's credibility to deceive targets who see "microsoft" in the address.

📧 Email Spoofing

Email clients do not always display the actual sending address. The visible "From" field can be set to any arbitrary value. A trusted name appears — even though the real sender address is completely different.

Modern email filtering can detect discrepancies between displayed and actual sender addresses — but not all environments have this configured, and not all users pay attention to the alerts.

Key Defense

  • Always examine the actual URL — not just the visible link text
  • Hover over links before clicking to reveal the real destination
  • Verify sender addresses carefully — especially in high-stakes requests
  • Never act on urgent financial or credential requests without a separate verbal confirmation
🎯 Business Email Compromise and Targeted Attacks

🎯 Business Email Compromise (BEC)

BEC is a highly targeted and sophisticated attack focusing on a specific individual — typically an executive, senior manager, or budget holder. The attacker poses as a trusted colleague, business partner, or vendor.

What Makes BEC Different

  • Requires detailed reconnaissance on the target
  • No obvious phishing indicators — no spoofed links, no suspicious attachments
  • Reads like legitimate business communication
  • Attacker may first compromise a real email account to increase credibility

Targeted Attack Terminology — Know These

Spear Phishing

Phishing directed at a specific, identified individual rather than a broad audience.

Whaling

Spear phishing targeting high-value individuals — executives and senior leadership.

CEO Fraud

Impersonating the CEO to pressure employees into authorizing fraudulent payments.

Angler Phishing

Targeted phishing using social media as the attack vector — often posing as customer service accounts.

🏔️ Watering Hole and Brand Impersonation

🏔️ Watering Hole Attack

Does not target the organization directly. Instead, the attacker compromises a third-party website the target group is known to visit regularly.

Name Origin: Like a predator waiting at the watering hole — instead of chasing prey, the attacker waits where prey must come to them.

How It Works

  • Attacker identifies a routine website used by employees (e.g., a local delivery service)
  • The third-party site is compromised — malicious exploit code is embedded
  • Employees visit as part of their normal routine — devices are infected silently
  • Infected devices become a pathway into the primary target's network
These attacks exploit trusted, routine behavior. The victim does nothing suspicious — they simply visit a site they use every day.

🎨 Brand Impersonation and Disinformation

The attacker accurately replicates a company's visual identity — logos, fonts, colors, heading styles — to make a phishing message or pharming website visually indistinguishable from the real thing.

Additional Tactics

  • Mimic the tone and style of legitimate email communications
  • Use realistic content to achieve high search engine ranking for the fake site
  • Create fake social media posts and referrer sites to boost visibility

Disinformation vs. Misinformation

Disinformation — deliberate and intentional creation and spread of false information to deceive.
Misinformation — repeating or amplifying false claims without intent to deceive. A disinformation campaign often seeds false information and relies on others to spread it as misinformation.

All Key Exam Terms from Lesson 2 — Comparing Threat Types
A
APT (Advanced Persistent Threat) Nation-State / Threat Actor Types

An adversary that gains and maintains long-term, stealthy access to a target network using a wide variety of tools and techniques. Focuses on sustained compromise rather than isolated intrusions.

Attack Surface Attack Vectors

The complete set of points in a system or network that are exposed to potential exploitation by a threat actor. Includes network ports, applications, endpoints, user accounts, and physical access points.

Attack Vector Attack Vectors

The specific method or pathway used by a threat actor to gain unauthorized access to a system. Often used interchangeably with threat vector, though some sources distinguish them by whether an exploit has been successfully executed.

Agentless Scanning Vulnerable Software Vectors

Vulnerability scanning conducted remotely without installing software on the target host. Preferred by threat actors during reconnaissance because it leaves minimal traces and requires no footprint on the target system.

Angler Phishing Social Engineering

Targeted phishing conducted using social media as the primary vector. Attackers may pose as customer service accounts to intercept and exploit complaints or inquiries from users.

B
BEC (Business Email Compromise) Social Engineering

A sophisticated, targeted attack impersonating a trusted contact — colleague, business partner, or vendor — to manipulate a specific individual within an organization. Characterized by detailed reconnaissance and absence of typical phishing indicators.

Backdoor Lure-Based Vectors

A hidden method of bypassing normal authentication to gain unauthorized access to a system. Commonly installed by Trojan Horse malware as the primary payload.

Blackmail Threat Motivations

Demanding payment to prevent the release of damaging information. The information may be real — stolen data — or fabricated to make the target appear guilty of a crime.

Brand Impersonation Social Engineering

Accurately replicating an organization's visual identity — logos, fonts, colors, layout, and communication tone — to make phishing content visually indistinguishable from legitimate communications.

Business Partner Supply Chain

A company with closely aligned goals sharing deeper access and integration with another organization. Greater access than a standard vendor relationship — and therefore a greater supply chain risk.

C
CEO Fraud Social Engineering

An attack where the threat actor impersonates the CEO — or another executive — to pressure employees into authorizing fraudulent wire transfers or payments.

Client-Based (Agent-Based) Scanning Vulnerable Software Vectors

Vulnerability scanning using software agents installed directly on each host. Provides deeper, more detailed visibility into each system compared to agentless approaches.

Commodity Tools Threat Actor Attributes

Widely available, pre-built attack tools used by lower-capability threat actors who lack the skill to develop their own exploits. A key indicator of a script kiddie or low-sophistication actor.

Competitor Threat Actor Threat Actor Types

A business or individual acting on behalf of a competing organization to steal trade secrets, disrupt operations, or damage a rival's reputation. Often facilitated through insider knowledge from recently hired employees.

Credential Harvesting Social Engineering

The capture of usernames and passwords through deceptive means — typically via spoofed websites or phishing campaigns that present fake login pages identical to legitimate ones.

CSP (Cloud Service Provider) Network Vectors

A third-party company providing cloud infrastructure and services. A potential target for attackers seeking access to client environments hosted on the provider's platform.

D
Data Exfiltration Threat Motivations / Attack Strategies

The unauthorized transfer of data out of a network. Targets the Confidentiality property of the CIA Triad. Data may be sold, leveraged for blackmail, or used for espionage.

Deepfake Social Engineering

AI-generated synthetic audio or video used to impersonate real individuals. An emerging threat in voice and video phishing — making vishing attacks increasingly convincing and harder to detect.

Default Credentials Network Vectors

Factory-set usernames and passwords left unchanged on a device or application after deployment. Publicly documented in product manuals — one of the most preventable and most commonly exploited vulnerabilities.

Disinformation Threat Motivations / Social Engineering

The deliberate and intentional creation and spread of false information to deceive. Distinguished from misinformation by the presence of deliberate intent. Targets the Integrity property of the CIA Triad.

DNS (Domain Name System) Social Engineering

The system that translates human-readable website addresses into IP addresses. A target of pharming attacks — corruption of DNS resolution silently redirects users from legitimate sites to malicious ones.

DoS (Denial of Service) Network Vectors

An attack that disrupts service availability to prevent legitimate users from accessing resources. Results from a lack of the Availability property in the CIA Triad. Service disruption as an attack strategy.

Drive-By Download Message-Based Vectors

An automatic malware infection triggered by visiting a compromised website. No deliberate user action — clicking or downloading — is needed. Simply visiting the page triggers the exploit in a vulnerable browser.

Drop Attack Lure-Based Vectors

A physical social engineering technique where the attacker leaves infected USB drives in accessible locations — parking lots, office lobbies, reception areas — expecting at least one employee to connect the device.

E
Eavesdropping Attack Network Vectors

Intercepting network traffic to capture passwords or sensitive data. Results from a lack of confidentiality on the network. Also known as sniffing or packet capture attacks.

Email Spoofing Social Engineering

Manipulating the "From" field of an email to display a trusted name or address — even though the actual sending address is completely different. Used to make phishing messages appear to come from legitimate sources.

Endpoint Security Management Lure-Based Vectors

The set of controls used to protect individual devices from exploitation — including vulnerability management, antivirus, program execution control, and intrusion detection. Essential defense against lure-based vector attacks.

Espionage Threat Motivations / Nation-State Actors

A type of data exfiltration aimed at gathering intelligence — learning secrets rather than selling them or using them for blackmail. A primary nation-state motivation conducted continuously, not only during active conflict.

Extortion Threat Motivations

Demanding payment to stop an ongoing attack or restore access to systems or data. Ransomware is the most common modern form of extortion — malware encrypts files and demands payment to restore them.

External Threat Actor Threat Actor Attributes

A threat actor who must gain unauthorized access to reach the target. Has no legitimate permissions before the attack begins. Must infiltrate through technical or physical means — the actor is external, not necessarily the attack location.

F
False Flag Operation Nation-State Actors

An attack designed to appear as though it originated from a different threat actor or nation. Makes attribution extremely difficult — defenders must be cautious about drawing quick conclusions.

Financial Fraud Threat Motivations

Deceptive schemes targeting individuals or organizations for financial gain. Includes falsifying records, embezzling funds, laundering money, and manipulating stock prices through disinformation.

H
Hacker (Authorized / Unauthorized) Threat Actor Types

An individual with the skills to access computer systems. Authorized (white hat) hackers seek explicit permission and work legally. Unauthorized (black hat) hackers access systems without permission for malicious or self-serving purposes.

Hacktivist Threat Actor Types

An individual or group that uses cyber attack techniques to advance a political or ideological agenda. Primary goal is not financial — it is to make a public statement or force behavioral change.

Human Vector Social Engineering

The collective group of people — employees, contractors, suppliers, and customers — who represent the human layer of an organization's attack surface. A person with permissions on a system is a potential target.

I
Impersonation Social Engineering

Pretending to be someone else to manipulate a target into revealing information or granting access. Most effective when the target cannot easily verify the attacker's identity — over the phone or via email.

Insider Threat Threat Actor Types

A threat actor who has been granted authorized access to organizational systems. Includes permanent insiders (employees) and temporary insiders (contractors, guests). Former employees are a blurred case requiring careful offboarding controls.

J
Jurisdiction Organized Crime

The legal authority of a government to prosecute crimes. A key challenge when cybercriminals operate across national borders — laws differ, evidence gathering is slow, and cooperation agreements are limited between some nations.

L
Local Exploit Network Vectors

An attack technique that requires execution from within an authenticated session on the target system. The attack may travel over a network — but valid credentials or a hijacked session are required first.

Lure Lure-Based Vectors

A deceptive file or device designed to appear legitimate while concealing a malicious payload. When the target opens the lure, the payload delivers the attack — typically granting attacker control or causing disruption.

M
Macro Lure-Based Vectors

A scripting feature built into document formats (Word, Excel, PDF) that can be exploited to execute malicious code when a file is opened. A common lure technique requiring no separate vulnerability in the OS.

Malicious Payload Lure-Based Vectors

The harmful code delivered by a lure that gives an attacker control over the compromised system or causes disruption. The "hook" hidden inside the bait of a lure-based attack.

Misinformation Social Engineering

Repeating or amplifying false claims without the intention to deceive. Distinguished from disinformation by the absence of deliberate deceptive intent. Disinformation campaigns often rely on misinformation spread to amplify their false narratives.

MITRE ATT&CK Nation-State Actors

A publicly available framework that catalogs the tactics, techniques, and procedures of known threat actor groups — including nation-state actors and organized crime. Used to map observed attack behaviors to known threat profiles.

MSP (Managed Service Provider) Supply Chain

A third-party company that manages IT resources on behalf of an organization. MSP relationships are complex security risks — monitoring is difficult, and all MSP employees are potential insider threats with authorized access to client environments.

Multistage Campaign Attack Surface and Vectors

A planned, sequential attack using multiple vectors to achieve a strategic objective. Characteristic of sophisticated threat actors — distinct from opportunistic "smash and grab" single-event attacks.

N
Nation-State Actor Threat Actor Types

A government-sponsored threat actor using cyber capabilities to achieve military, political, or economic objectives. Characterized by high capability, significant resources, and plausible deniability from the sponsoring government.

Novel Vector Attack Surface and Vectors

A new or previously unknown attack path developed by a highly capable threat actor. Defenders may not have identified or protected against these paths — a significant risk when facing sophisticated adversaries.

O
On-Path Attack Network Vectors

An attack where the threat actor intercepts and potentially modifies traffic between two communicating parties. Results from a lack of integrity on the network. Previously known as man-in-the-middle (MitM) attacks.

Open Service Port Network Vectors

A TCP or UDP port accepting unauthenticated connections. Applications listening on unnecessary open ports expand the attack surface. Servers should never be configured to accept traffic on ports not required for operations.

Organized Crime Threat Actor Types

Structured criminal groups that use cyber methods to generate profit through fraud, blackmail, and extortion. Operate across national borders — making prosecution legally complex. In many countries, cybercrime has surpassed physical crime in total incidents and losses.

P
Patch Management Vulnerable Software Vectors

The process of identifying, acquiring, testing, and applying software updates to close known vulnerabilities. Typically a preventive control — but becomes corrective when applied after a vulnerability has already been exploited.

Pharming Social Engineering

An attack that corrupts the DNS name resolution process to silently redirect users from legitimate websites to malicious ones. The user types the correct address but is redirected without any suspicious link being clicked.

Phishing Social Engineering

A combination of social engineering and spoofing that tricks targets into interacting with a malicious resource disguised as a trusted one. Delivered primarily via email. May harvest credentials via spoofed websites or deliver malware via attachments.

Physical Intrusion Social Engineering / Network Vectors

Gaining unauthorized physical access to a facility as part of an attack. Can be facilitated through social engineering — creating diversions such as fire alarms — to slip past physical controls undetected.

Plausible Deniability Nation-State Actors

The deliberate separation between a sponsoring government and its cyber operators to allow official denial of involvement if the attack is attributed. State actors work at arm's length from the governments that fund them.

Pretexting Social Engineering

The use of a fabricated, detailed scenario to make an impersonation more convincing and credible. The attacker builds a believable context using specific names, titles, internal processes, and organizational details.

Procurement Management Supply Chain

The process of ensuring that equipment and software are sourced from reliable and trustworthy suppliers. Involves evaluating supplier, vendor, and business partner relationships for security risk.

R
Reconnaissance Threat Actors / Vulnerable Software

The information-gathering phase of an attack in which a threat actor maps the target's systems, personnel, and vulnerabilities. Social engineering and agentless scanning are frequently used to support this phase.

Remote Exploit Network Vectors

An attack technique that triggers a vulnerability over a network without requiring an authenticated session. The attacker does not need valid credentials to launch the attack — the code is sent across the network to trigger the flaw.

S
Script Kiddie Threat Actor Types

A low-capability threat actor who uses pre-built, widely available tools without deep technical understanding. Attacks are often untargeted — motivated by attention or curiosity. Even low-skill actors can cause damage against weak defenses.

Service Disruption Threat Motivations / Attack Strategies

Preventing an organization from operating normally. Can be an end goal in itself — causing chaos or revenge — or a tactic supporting another objective. Targets the Availability property of the CIA Triad.

Shadow IT Insider Threats

Unauthorized hardware or software introduced into the workplace without IT department knowledge or approval. Creates unmonitored attack surfaces. Exacerbated by the proliferation of cloud services and personal mobile devices.

SMiShing Social Engineering / Message-Based Vectors

Phishing conducted through SMS text messaging. The SS7 protocol underlying SMS has known vulnerabilities. Organizations typically have no monitoring capability for SMS — making threats largely invisible to enterprise security tools.

Social Engineering Social Engineering

Manipulative techniques used by threat actors to exploit human behavior and psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. Can be used for reconnaissance — eliciting information — or intrusion — obtaining credentials or access.

Spear Phishing Social Engineering

A targeted phishing attack directed at a specific, identified individual rather than a broad audience. Requires reconnaissance — the attacker studies the target's role, relationships, and communication patterns.

Spoofed Website Social Engineering

A fake website designed to look identical to a legitimate one for the purpose of credential harvesting. Users authenticate with the spoofed site — their login credentials are captured by the attacker.

Spoofing Network Vectors

Impersonating a trusted resource — such as a wireless access point — to deceive users into connecting. The spoofed resource harvests credentials from connecting users, which are then used to access the real network.

SS7 (Signaling System 7) Message-Based Vectors

The protocol underlying SMS text messaging. Associated with numerous known vulnerabilities that allow traffic interception. A weak link in mobile communication security and a target for sophisticated attackers.

Structured / Targeted Threat Threat Actor Attributes

A deliberate, focused attack against a specific organization or individual. The attacker plans and executes a campaign against a chosen target — contrasted with unstructured, opportunistic attacks.

Supply Chain Attack Supply Chain

An attack that targets a less-defended supplier, vendor, or partner as a pathway into the primary target organization. Rather than attacking the target directly, the attacker compromises a trusted third party first.

T
Threat Actor / Threat Agent Core Threat Concepts

Any individual, group, or entity that poses a deliberate threat to an organization's systems or data. Threat actors are profiled by their location, capability, resources, and motivation.

Threat Vector Attack Surface and Vectors

The path or method used by a threat actor to execute an attack — including data exfiltration, service disruption, and disinformation. Often used interchangeably with attack vector.

Trojan Horse Malware Lure-Based Vectors

Malicious software disguised as a legitimate, useful program. Upon execution, it runs a hidden process that installs a backdoor — giving the attacker unauthorized remote access to the compromised system.

TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures) Core Threat Concepts

The behavioral profile of a threat actor describing how they plan and execute attacks. Tactics = overall goals; Techniques = specific methods; Procedures = step-by-step execution. Learning TTPs enables proactive rather than reactive defense.

Typosquatting (Cousin / Lookalike / Doppelganger Domain) Social Engineering

Registering a domain name that closely mimics a legitimate one through subtle misspellings or character substitutions. Used to host spoofed websites or send phishing emails that appear to come from a trusted source.

U
Unintentional Insider Threat Insider Threats

Harm caused by an insider through carelessness or lack of security awareness — not malicious intent. Common examples: poor password management, policy violations, and shadow IT.

Unstructured / Opportunistic Threat Threat Actor Attributes

An indiscriminate attack with no specific target in mind. The attacker exploits whatever opportunities are available — contrasted with structured, targeted campaigns that focus on a specific organization or individual.

Unsupported System Vulnerable Software Vectors

A system or application no longer receiving vendor updates or security patches. Known vulnerabilities exist — but no official fix will ever be released. Requires isolation as a compensating control if replacement is not possible.

V
Vishing Social Engineering / Message-Based Vectors

Phishing conducted over a voice channel — telephone or VoIP. Harder to refuse a live voice request than an email. Deepfake technology is increasing the sophistication and believability of vishing attacks.

Vulnerability Core Threat Concepts

A weakness in a system or process that could be accidentally triggered or intentionally exploited to cause a security breach. Severity is determined by the value of the vulnerable asset and the ease of exploitation.

Vulnerable Software Vulnerable Software Vectors

Software containing a code or design flaw that can be exploited to bypass access controls or crash running processes. One of the most commonly exploited threat vectors due to the complexity of modern software and patching delays.

W
Watering Hole Attack Social Engineering

Compromising a third-party website known to be used by the target group — infecting their devices indirectly. Exploits trusted, routine behavior — victims do nothing suspicious, they simply visit a site they use every day.

Whaling Social Engineering

Spear phishing aimed at high-value targets — executives, directors, and senior leadership — who have significant organizational influence and financial authority.

Whistleblower Insider Threats

An insider who exposes organizational wrongdoing for ethical reasons through authorized channels. Legally protected when making protected disclosures — cannot be labeled, threatened, or penalized in a retaliatory manner.

Zero-Click Exploit Message-Based Vectors

An attack that executes simply by receiving a message or file — requiring no user interaction beyond receipt. Removes the human decision-making layer entirely. Even trained, security-aware users cannot prevent zero-click attacks through behavior alone.

🎯 Exam Prep Tips — Lesson 2

  • Be able to identify threat actor types from scenario descriptions — motivation and capability are the key differentiators.
  • Know the Risk formula: Risk = Likelihood × Impact. Be able to apply it to a scenario.
  • Distinguish between the three attack strategies — service disruption (availability), data exfiltration (confidentiality), disinformation (integrity).
  • Know all phishing variants: phishing (email), vishing (voice), SMiShing (SMS), pharming (DNS), and their key differences.
  • Understand what makes BEC different from mass phishing — it is targeted, researched, and avoids obvious indicators.
  • Be able to define TTPs and explain why they are more useful than signature-based detection alone.
  • Know the difference between disinformation (deliberate) and misinformation (unintentional).
  • Understand shadow IT and why it creates an unmonitored attack surface — a common exam scenario topic.
